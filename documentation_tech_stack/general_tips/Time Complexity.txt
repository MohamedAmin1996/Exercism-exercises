Here is the complete order of time complexity from best (fastest) to worst (slowest), including the multi-variable ones we just talked about.

To help visualize how these scale as your data grows, think of "best" as the algorithms that keep your CPU cool and happy, and "worst" as the ones that will freeze your application or crash your server.

---

### The Ultimate Big O Hierarchy (Best to Worst)

1.O(1) — Constant Time** (The Absolute Best)
* Why:* It takes the exact same fraction of a second regardless of data size.
* C# Example:* `myDictionary["key"]` or checking `list.Count`.


2. $O(log n) — Logarithmic Time** (Excellent)
* *Why:* The execution time grows incredibly slowly because the problem space is halved at every step.
* *C# Example:* `Array.BinarySearch()` on a sorted array.


3. O(n) — Linear Time** (Good/Fair)
* *Why:* Time grows in direct, predictable proportion to the data size. If data doubles, time doubles.
* *C# Example:* A standard `foreach` loop or LINQ's `.Count(predicate)`.


4. O(n + m) — Linear Multi-Variable Time** (Good/Fair)
* *Why:* It behaves just like linear time, but accounts for two independent datasets processed one after the other.
* *C# Example:* Looping through a list of Users, then looping through a separate list of Products.


5. O(n \log n) — Linearithmic / Log-Linear Time** (Decent)
* *Why:* Slightly slower than pure linear time, but it is the mathematical gold standard for efficiently ordering randomized data.
* *C# Example:* `Array.Sort()` or LINQ's `.OrderBy()`.


6. O(n \times m) — Quadratic Multi-Variable Time** (Sub-optimal)
* *Why:* Slower than linear, but its actual speed heavily depends on the size of the smaller variable (m).
* *C# Example:* Nested loops where the outer loop is dataset n and the inner loop is dataset m.


7. O(n^2) — Quadratic Time** (Poor)
* *Why:* Time grows at an accelerating rate because every single item must be compared to every other item in the *same* dataset.
* *C# Example:* A classic Bubble Sort, or nested `for` loops iterating over the same collection size.


8. O(n^x) — Polynomial Time** (Bad)
* *Why:* Tripled (cubic O(n^3)) or quadrupled nested loops. It becomes rapidly unusable for larger datasets.
* *C# Example:* Checking every combination of triplets in a list.


9. O(2^n) — Exponential Time** (Terrible)
* *Why:* The operations double with every *single* new item added to the input. It hits a performance wall incredibly fast.
* *C# Example:* Unoptimized recursive Fibonacci algorithms.


10. O(n!) — Factorial Time** (The Absolute Worst)
* *Why:* It explodes so violently that even a tiny input size of n=20 could take literal years to finish processing on a supercomputer.
* *C# Example:* Generating every single possible permutation of a string or array.



---

### A Quick Rule of Thumb for C# Devs

When you are writing code, your goal should almost always be to stay **at or above O(n log n)**.

If you find yourself slipping into O(n x m) or O(n^2), it’s a massive red flag. Often, you can use a C# `HashSet<T>` or `Dictionary<TKey, TValue>` to trade a little bit of memory space to instantly drop a slow O(n^2) algorithm down to an incredibly fast O(n) or O(n + m) algorithm!
